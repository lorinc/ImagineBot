ImagineBot — Evaluation Report

How well does the system answer questions from school policy documents? · 2026-04-15

What this system does: ImagineBot reads school policy documents and answers questions about them. When a question arrives, the system first figures out which sections of the relevant document are needed, then retrieves exactly those sections and passes them to an AI to write the answer. Nothing is guessed from memory — every answer is grounded in the actual document text.

What this report shows: We ran the system against a set of test questions where we already knew the correct answers and which document sections were needed. We then measured how accurately the system found the right sections and how well the answers matched the expected content.

Glossary — terms used in this report

Section retrieval
Finding the right part of a document to answer a question.
Recall (completeness)
Did the system find all the sections needed? 100% = nothing missed.
Precision (efficiency)
Of the sections retrieved, how many were actually needed? 100% = no unnecessary sections fetched.
F1 score
A single number combining recall and precision. 100% = perfect on both. We care more about recall — missing a section is worse than fetching one extra.
Document routing
For questions that might span multiple documents, the system first decides which document(s) to look in. This step is tested separately.
Hard queries
Test questions specifically designed to be tricky — requiring cross-section reasoning, rare vocabulary, or information spread across multiple parts of a document.
Standard queries
Everyday questions a parent or staff member might ask. Simpler, but still representative of real usage.
Ground truth
The known-correct answer used to evaluate the system. Only the hard queries have ground truth labels; standard queries are shown for review but not automatically scored.

P1H5 Staff are concerned about a student — she seems withdrawn, has unexplained bruising, and hasn't been eating. What should they do and what specific signs are they looking for? ✓ All required sections found

Staff should take the following actions:

Staff have a moral and statutory responsibility to safeguard and promote the welfare of all pupils [1.3]. They must be alert to the signs of abuse and neglect and follow the school's procedures [1.3, 1.4]. All staff have an equal responsibility to act on any suspicion that a child is at risk of harm [1.3]. When concerned about a child's welfare, staff members should always act in the interests of the child [1.3].

It is the responsibility of staff to report their concerns, and they do not need 'absolute proof' that the child is at risk [1.11.L21]. They should report all concerns, however minor or insignificant they may seem [1.11.L21]. Staff must report their concerns to the Designated Safeguarding Lead (DSL) [1.11.L21, 1.13, 1.16] or, in their absence, the deputy Designated Safeguarding Lead [1.16]. It is not their responsibility to investigate or decide whether a child has been abused [1.11.L21].

The school will normally seek to discuss concerns with parents, but if notifying parents could increase the risk to the child, advice will be sought first from Children's Social Care [1.13]. The school will also seek advice from Children's Social Care when they have reasonable cause to suspect a child may be suffering or likely to suffer significant harm [1.1].

The specific signs staff are looking for, based on the student's presentation, include:

  • Withdrawn: This is an indicator of abuse, specifically a "change in behaviour - from quiet to aggressive, or happy-go-lucky to withdrawn" [1.11.L21].
  • Unexplained bruising: This is a physical indicator of abuse, listed as "bruises, bleeding, burns, fractures, or other injuries" [1.11.L21]. Physical abuse may involve "hitting... or otherwise causing physical harm to a child" [1.11.L3].
  • Hasn't been eating: This is an indicator of abuse, specifically a "change their eating habits" [1.11.L21]. It is also a sign of neglect, which is defined as the "persistent failure to meet a child's basic physical and/or psychological needs," including a parent or carer failing to "Provide adequate food" [1.11.L12].

P1H6 What is the difference between how the school responds when a student actively discloses abuse versus when staff only suspect it based on signs? ✓ All required sections found

When a student actively discloses abuse, the staff member engages directly with the student by allowing them to speak freely, remaining calm, offering reassuring words, and explaining that the information must be passed on (as staff cannot keep secrets) [1.13]. The staff member should avoid asking investigative questions but can ask clarifying ones, and should not offer physical touch as comfort or admonish the child for not disclosing earlier [1.13]. The staff member must tell the pupil what will happen next and that someone (staff or DSL) will see them before the end of the day, then report verbally to the Designated Safeguarding Lead (DSL) [1.13].

When staff only suspect abuse based on signs, their responsibility is to report their concerns to the Designated Safeguarding Lead (DSL); they are explicitly told not to investigate or decide whether a child has been abused [1.11.L21]. Staff should report all concerns, however minor, without needing "absolute proof" that the child is at risk [1.11.L21].

In both situations (disclosure or suspicion), the DSL will normally seek to discuss concerns with parents, but will first seek advice from Children's Social Care if notifying parents could increase the risk to the child [1.13].

2 What happens step-by-step when a bullying incident is reported to the school? Standard

When a bullying incident is reported to the school, the following steps are taken:

1. Reporting the Incident

  • Students and families should report any suspicion of bullying immediately to a Tutor, Key Stage Manager, or Head teacher [6.10].
  • Staff who observe or are told about bullying must pass their concerns to other staff working with the child and report these concerns to the Head Teacher [6.10].
  • Pupils witnessing bullying should support the child being bullied and encourage them to speak out, or with their permission, speak to a trusted adult on their behalf [6.10].
  • Parents should bring the issue to the attention of any member of staff, the Head of School, or the named person responsible for supporting good behaviour [6.10].

2. Initial Staff Response and Recording

  • If a staff member receives a report, they reassure and support the pupils involved [6.10].
  • Staff advise pupils that details will be passed on to their Guide and/or the named person responsible for supporting good behaviour (Key Stage Coordinator/Head Teacher) [6.10].
  • The named person keeps a log of all complaints or incidences of bullying and records how they were dealt with [6.10].
  • Details of the incident are recorded by the welfare responsible (Lindsay &/or Sheila) [6.12].
  • The Guide discusses with the named person for behaviour support what the appropriate and most helpful course of action is [6.12].

3. Interviewing Involved Parties

  • The child who has been bullied is interviewed by their Guide, who may ask them to write an immediate account of events and discuss their reaction. The process for dealing with the incident is clearly explained, and further support and advice may be given [6.11.L0].
  • Once it is clear that a bullying incident has occurred, the child responsible for the bullying and any others related to the incident are interviewed. Each is asked to write an immediate account of events, and the process for dealing with the incident is clearly explained to them [6.11.L0].

4. Investigation and Decision on Actions/Consequences

  • Before issuing a formal warning, the adult who witnesses the incident, the Guide, and the Head Teacher investigate the bullying incident to gather facts, speak with witnesses, and understand the context [6.11.L12].
  • Sanctions may be applied in accordance with the school’s Behaviour Policy [6.11.L8].
  • This may include a formal bullying warning, clearly stating what the bullying incident was and what would constitute a continuation [6.12].
  • Clear strategies on what work would help both children (or groups of children) to be kind and loving towards each other may be developed [6.12].

5. Formal School Warning Process (if applicable)

  • Families Informed: Clear and timely communication with the families of both the bully and the victim occurs as soon as possible after the incident has been reported and investigated, typically via phone call, followed by a written notice [6.11.L12]. Meetings may be arranged to discuss the incident, share findings, and explain actions [6.11.L12]. Ongoing communication is maintained [6.11.L12].
  • Warning Issued: The school issues the formal warning, which may include a written notice outlining the incident, suspension, or exclusion, and is documented in the student's record [6.11.L12].
  • Expectations and Consequences: The student is informed of the expectations for their future behaviour and the consequences they will face if they do not adhere to these expectations [6.11.L12].

6. Ongoing Monitoring and Review

M5 What is the correct procedure when a student discloses abuse to a teacher? Multi-doc

When a student discloses abuse to a member of staff or volunteer, the following procedure should be followed:

1. The staff member must inform the pupil that they cannot keep secrets and must pass the information on, exercising professional judgment on the timing of this disclosure [1.13].

2. During the conversation, the staff member should allow the pupil to speak freely, remain calm, and offer reassuring words such as "I'm so sorry this has happened," "I want to help," "This isn't your fault," or "You are doing the right thing in talking to me" [1.13].

3. The staff member should not be afraid of silences and must under no circumstances ask investigative questions, though clarifying questions (e.g., "when did this happen," "where did this happen") are reasonable to support a meaningful referral [1.13].

4. The staff member should not automatically offer physical touch as comfort and should avoid admonishing the child for not disclosing earlier [1.13].

5. The staff member must tell the pupil what will happen next and let them know that someone (either the staff member or another named person, such as the DSL) will come to see them before the end of the day [1.13].

6. The staff member must report verbally to the Designated Safeguarding Lead (DSL) [1.13].

7. The staff member should seek support if they feel distressed or need to debrief [1.13].

8. The school will normally seek to discuss any concerns with parents, with the DSL making contact. However, if the school believes that notifying parents could increase the risk to the child or exacerbate the problem, advice will be sought first from Children's Social Care [1.13].
