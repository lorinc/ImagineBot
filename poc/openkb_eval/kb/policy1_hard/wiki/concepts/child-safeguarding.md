# Child Safeguarding

[[Child Safeguarding]] refers to the comprehensive process of protecting children from maltreatment, preventing the impairment of their health or development, ensuring they grow up in circumstances consistent with the provision of safe and effective care, and taking action to enable all children to achieve the best outcomes.

This concept is a cornerstone of the school's operations, as detailed in [[summaries/policy1_hard]], which outlines the school's commitment and procedures.

## Core Principles
As established in the school's policy, several principles underpin effective [[Child Safeguarding]]:

*   **Paramount Importance:** The school's responsibility to safeguard and promote the welfare of children is of the highest priority.
*   **Equal Rights to Protection:** All children, regardless of age, gender, ability, culture, race, language, religion, or sexual identity, have an equal right to protection.
*   **Safe Learning Environment:** Children who feel safe and secure are better equipped to learn and thrive.
*   **Shared Commitment:** All staff and volunteers are expected to share the commitment to safeguarding and promoting the welfare of children.
*   **Proactive Stance:** Staff must maintain an attitude of 'It could happen here' and act in the best interests of the child when concerns arise.

## Legal and Regulatory Framework
[[Child Safeguarding]] is governed by a robust legal and regulatory framework to ensure compliance and best practice. Key legislation and guidance referenced in the school's policy include:

*   **Children Act 1989 (UK):** Requires school staff to pass on information concerning a child's welfare.
*   **Education Act 2002 & Independent School Standards Regulations 2014:** Mandate independent schools to have arrangements for safeguarding and promoting child welfare.
*   **Teacher Standards 2012:** Require teachers to safeguard pupils' well-being.
*   ***[[Working Together to Safeguard Children]]*** (DfE 2015/2024): Statutory guidance covering legislative requirements and expectations for services, including schools.
*   ***[[Keeping Children Safe in Education]]*** (DfE 2016/2024): Statutory guidance for schools on safeguarding duties, requiring all staff to read specific sections.
*   ***What to do if you're worried a child is being abused*** (HMG 2015): Non-statutory advice for practitioners on identifying and acting on abuse and [[Neglect]].

## Roles and Responsibilities
Effective [[Child Safeguarding]] relies on clear roles and responsibilities:

*   **All Staff:** Due to day-to-day contact with pupils, all school staff are uniquely placed to observe changes in behavior and signs of harm. They must be alert to signs of [[Child Abuse]], approachable, listen actively, and understand reporting procedures. Staff are required to read the [[Child Protection Policy]] and Staff Behaviour Policy.
*   **[[Designated Safeguarding Lead (DSL)]]:** A senior member of staff with lead responsibility for [[Child Protection]] in the school. The DSL acts as a source of support and expertise, maintains records, refers cases to social care or police, ensures policy reviews, and provides training. The DSL coordinates the school's contribution to [[Child Protection]] plans and ensures public availability of the policy.

## Recognizing and Responding to Concerns
[[Child Safeguarding]] procedures involve:

*   **Recognizing Abuse:** Staff are trained to understand the four categories of [[Types of Child Abuse]]: [[Physical Abuse]], [[Emotional Abuse]], [[Sexual Abuse]], and [[Neglect]]. Definitions are derived from *[[Working Together to Safeguard Children]]*.
*   **Indicators of Abuse:** Staff are educated on physical and behavioral indicators of abuse, emphasizing that individual indicators are part of a larger picture. Staff are responsible for reporting concerns, not for investigating them.
*   **Disclosure Procedures:** If a pupil discloses abuse, staff must allow them to speak freely, remain calm, offer comfort, avoid investigative questions, and inform the pupil that the information must be passed on to the DSL. Physical touch for comfort is avoided in such situations.
*   **Notifying Parents:** The school generally discusses concerns with parents, but advice from Children's Social Care is sought first if notification could increase risk to the child.

## Specific Safeguarding Areas
*   **[[Children Missing from Education]]:** The policy highlights the risks associated with children missing education (e.g., exploitation, radicalization) and outlines strict monitoring of attendance, with immediate referrals to social services for prolonged unexplained absences.
*   **Educating Children on Safety:** Pupils are taught about [[Safeguarding]], including [[Online Safety]], managing risk, consent, healthy relationships, and preventing bullying (including cyberbullying) and [[Sexual Exploitation of Children]] (including [[Sexting]]) as part of a broad and balanced curriculum (e.g., PSHE and Sex and Relationships education).
*   **Complaints Procedure:** Concerns about poor practice that do not meet the [[Child Protection]] threshold are handled via the school's complaints procedure, with an escalation to [[Child Protection]] procedures if concerns reach that threshold.

## Inter-Agency Collaboration
The school is committed to an inter-agency approach, developing effective liaison with other agencies to ensure children receive timely and appropriate support. This includes contributing to [[Child Protection]] conferences and sharing information effectively with relevant professionals.

## Monitoring and Review
The [[Child Protection Policy]] is reviewed at least annually to ensure it remains current with legislation and best practice, often involving the whole school community, including pupils and parents.
