# Types of Child Abuse

Child abuse refers to various forms of maltreatment, identified and defined to ensure the protection and welfare of children. Understanding these categories is fundamental for any [[child protection policy]] and for staff to effectively identify and report concerns. The document [[summaries/policy1_hard]] outlines four primary types of abuse, with definitions primarily drawn from *[[Working Together to Safeguard Children]]*.

## Categories of Child Abuse

### 1. Physical Abuse
Physical abuse involves deliberately causing physical harm to a child. This can manifest in various ways, including:
*   Hitting, shaking, throwing
*   Poisoning, burning or scalding, drowning, suffocating
*   Other acts causing physical injury

It also includes instances where a parent or carer fabricates the symptoms of, or deliberately induces, illness in a child, formerly known as Munchausen's Syndrome by Proxy and now referred to as fabricated or induced illness.

### 2. Emotional Abuse
Emotional abuse is characterized by persistent emotional maltreatment that causes severe and lasting adverse effects on a child's emotional development. This can involve:
*   Conveying to a child that they are worthless, unloved, inadequate, or only valued for meeting another's needs.
*   Not providing opportunities for a child to express views, deliberately silencing them, or mocking their communication.
*   Imposing age or developmentally inappropriate expectations, including overprotection or limiting exploration and social interaction.
*   Exposing a child to or letting them witness the ill-treatment of another.
*   Serious bullying (including [[cyberbullying]]), causing frequent fear or danger.
*   The exploitation or corruption of children.

Emotional abuse often underlies other forms of maltreatment but can also occur independently.

### 3. Sexual Abuse
Sexual abuse involves forcing or enticing a child or young person to participate in sexual activities, regardless of whether the child is aware of the full nature of what is happening. This can include both physical contact and non-contact activities:
*   **Physical Contact:** Assault by penetration (e.g., rape, oral sex), or non-penetrative acts such as masturbation, kissing, rubbing, and touching outside of clothing.
*   **Non-Contact Activities:** Involving children in looking at or producing sexual images, watching sexual activities, encouraging sexually inappropriate behaviors, or [[grooming]] a child in preparation for abuse (including via the internet).

Sexual abuse can be perpetrated by adults of any gender or by other children.

### 4. Neglect
[[Neglect]] is the persistent failure to meet a child's basic physical and/or psychological needs, which is likely to result in serious impairment of the child's health or development. It can begin during pregnancy due to maternal substance abuse. Once a child is born, neglect may involve a parent or carer failing to:
*   Provide adequate food, clothing, and shelter (including exclusion from home or abandonment).
*   Protect a child from physical and emotional harm or danger.
*   Ensure adequate supervision (including the use of inadequate caregivers).
*   Ensure access to appropriate medical care or treatment.
*   Be responsive to a child's basic emotional needs.

## Indicators of Abuse

While physical signs like bruises or broken bones are important, they can be hard to categorize or may be hidden. Therefore, staff must also be aware of a range of behavioral indicators. These include changes in behavior (e.g., withdrawn, aggressive), self-harm, frequent school absence, fearfulness, or displaying sexual knowledge beyond their age. It is crucial for staff to report *all* concerns, however minor they may seem, as individual indicators rarely provide conclusive evidence on their own; rather, they form part of a larger picture that the [[Designated Safeguarding Lead (DSL)]] will assess. Staff are responsible for reporting, not for investigating or determining if abuse has occurred.
