# **2. Dress Code Policy** {#dress-code-policy}

## **2.1. Scope of the Policy** {#scope-of-the-policy}

At Imagine we promote cultural diversity and value the differences students, staff and families bring to our community. We do not use uniform in the day-to-day of the school as part of our respect for individual expression. However, it is essential we impose a few justified gender-neutral dress code restrictions in order to keep everyone safe and allow them to focus on their work day.

Considering the international community and the variety of cultures we follow certain dress key principles. These principles help us to preserve our cultural diversity and ensure that every member of our community feels valued and able to express themselves.

## **2.2. Terminology** {#terminology}

*   **Dress Code**: Guidelines established to define acceptable attire for students and staff in the school environment.
*   **Health and Safety**: Measures taken to ensure the physical safety and wellbeing of all members of the school community.
*   **Cultural Diversity**: The inclusion and celebration of varied cultural backgrounds, practices, and expressions within the school.
*   **Gender-Neutral**: A policy or practice that does not differentiate based on gender.
*   **Religious Headwear**: Items worn for religious purposes, such as hijabs, turbans, or kippahs.

## **2.3. Policy Statement and Principles** {#policy-statement-and-principles}

At Imagine, we are committed to fostering an environment that celebrates cultural diversity and promotes respect for individual expression while maintaining safety and a focus on educational priorities. This dress code policy adheres to the following key principles:

1.  **Inclusivity**: We respect the cultural, religious, and personal choices of our community members. Our guidelines ensure everyone feels welcome and valued.
2.  **Safety**: Clothing and accessories must support the safety of students and staff, particularly during physical activities or in environments where hazards may exist.
3.  **Practicality**: Attire should enable ease of movement and comfort to facilitate participation in all school activities.
4.  **Modesty and Respect**: Guidelines aim to maintain a balance between individual expression and standards appropriate for a shared learning environment.
5.  **Clarity**: This policy provides clear, gender-neutral expectations to prevent misunderstandings or confusion.

# **3. Attendance Policy** {#attendance-policy}

## **3.1. Scope** {#scope}

Imagine Montessori School values the importance of attendance and punctuality. Children benefit from regular attendance as they adapt to the routines of the learning environment. Imagine Montessori School recognizes its responsibilities to ensure pupils are in school and on time, therefore having access to learning for the maximum number of days and hours.

Our policy applies to all children registered at this school, and this policy is made available to all parents/carers of pupils.

This policy has been written to adhere to the relevant Children Acts, Education Acts, Regulations, and Guidance from the Department for Education.

From the age of 6, parents have the legal responsibility to ensure their child’s good attendance. The administration team works in conjunction with families to ensure that all pupils develop good attendance habits. Procedures in this policy are followed to ensure this happens.

In Early Years (0 to 11 years-old), the morning work cycle begins at 9:00. Children who are persistently late or absent soon fall behind with their learning. Children who are absent from school frequently develop large gaps in their learning which potentially may impact on their progress and their ability to meet age-related learning expectations, especially from Year 1 and above. With children aged 2-6, we are building on their sense of responsibility, routines, and self-confidence, which is why we stress good attendance.

## **3.2. Terminology** {#terminology-1}

*   **Attendance**: Refers to the presence of students at school during scheduled learning hours.
*   **Punctuality**: The act of arriving on time for school and scheduled classes.
*   **Authorised Absence**: A student’s absence that has been approved by the school for legitimate reasons, such as illness or medical appointments.
*   **Unauthorised Absence**: An absence not approved by the school, even if supported by the parent or carer.
*   **Persistent Absence**: Repeated absences that significantly affect a student’s attendance rate and overall learning.
*   **Morning Work Cycle**: The core learning period beginning at 9:00 AM for Early Years students.
*   **Transparent Classroom/Phidias**: The school’s digital systems used for recording attendance, lateness, and other student-related data.

## **3.3. Policy Statement and Principles** {#policy-statement-and-principles-1}

This attendance policy aims to ensure that all staff and families at our school are fully aware of and clear about the actions necessary to promote good attendance.

Through this Policy, we aim to:

*   Improve pupils’ achievement by ensuring high levels of attendance and punctuality.
*   Create an ethos in which good attendance and punctuality are recognised as the norm and seen to be valued by the school.
*   Raise awareness among parents and children of the importance of uninterrupted attendance and punctuality at every stage of a child’s education.
*   Ensure that our policy applies to Nursery and Reception aged children to promote good habits at an early age.
*   Work in partnership with parents so that all pupils realise their potential, unhindered by unnecessary absence.
*   Promote a positive and welcoming atmosphere in which pupils feel safe, secure, and valued, and encourage in pupils a sense of their own responsibility.
*   Establish a pattern of monitoring attendance and ensure consistency in recognising achievement and dealing with difficulties.
*   Recognise the key role of all staff, but especially class teachers, in promoting good attendance. We maintain and promote good attendance and punctuality through:
    *   Raising awareness of attendance and punctuality issues among all staff, parents, and children.
    *   Ensuring that parents understand the responsibility placed on them for making sure their child attends regularly and punctually.
    *   Equipping children with the life skills needed to take responsibility for good school attendance and punctuality, appropriate to the child’s age and development.
    *   Maintaining effective means of communication with parents, pupils, and staff on school attendance matters.
    *   Developing and implementing procedures for identifying, reporting, and reviewing cases of poor attendance and persistent lateness.
    *   Supporting pupils who have been experiencing any difficulties at home or at school which are preventing good attendance.
    *   Developing and implementing procedures to follow up non-attendance at school.

## **3.4. Definitions** {#definitions}

**a. Authorised Absence**

*   An absence is classified as authorised when a child has been away from school for a legitimate reason and the school has received notification from a parent or carer. For example, if a child has been unwell and the parent telephones the school to explain the absence. The school has two categories of Authorised Absence: “Sick Absence” and “Excused Absence”.
*   Only the school can make an absence authorised. Parents do not have this authority. Consequently, not all absences supported by parents will be classified as authorised.

**b. Unauthorised Absence**

*   An absence is classified as unauthorised when a child is away from school without the permission of the school.
*   Therefore, the absence is unauthorised if a child is away from school without good reason, even with the support of a parent.

**c. Procedures**

Our school will undertake to follow the following procedures to support good attendance:

*   To maintain appropriate registration processes.
*   To maintain appropriate attendance data.
*   To communicate clearly the attendance procedures and expectations to all staff, parents, and children.
*   To have consistent and systematic daily records which give details of any absence and lateness.
*   To follow up absences and persistent lateness if parents/carers have not communicated with the school.
*   To inform parents/carers what constitutes authorised and unauthorised absence.
*   To work with parents to improve individual pupil attendance and punctuality.
*   All staff should be aware that they must raise any attendance or punctuality concerns to the administration team responsible for monitoring attendance.

**d. Responsibilities**

All members of school staff have a responsibility for identifying trends in attendance and punctuality. The following includes a more specific list of the kinds of responsibilities individuals might have.

**e. Class Teacher**

Class teachers are responsible for:

*   Keeping an overview of class and individual attendance, looking particularly for either poor overall attendance, anomalies in patterns of attendance, and/or unusual explanations for attendance offered by children and their parents/carers.
*   Informing the Head Teacher where there are concerns and acting upon them.
*   Providing background information to support referrals.
*   Monitoring follow-up once actions have been taken to correct attendance concerns.
*   Emphasising with their class the importance of good attendance and promptness.
*   Following up absences with immediate requests for explanation, which should be noted in Transparent Classroom as a side note.
*   Discussing attendance issues at parent pedagogical sessions where necessary.

**f. Head Teacher**

The Head Teacher is responsible for:

*   Overall monitoring of school attendance on a termly basis.
*   Assessing trends in authorised and unauthorised absence.
*   Contacting families where concerns are raised about absence, including arranging meetings to discuss attendance issues.
*   Monitoring individual attendance where concerns have been raised.

**g. Administration Staff**

Staff in the School Office are responsible for:

*   Collating and recording registration and attendance information.
*   Taking and recording messages from parents regarding absence.
*   Ensuring the Absence/Late Book is completed.
*   Filling in the Transparent Classroom or Phidias for late arrivals and early dismissals.
*   Contacting parents of absent children where no contact has been made.

**h. Parents**

Parents/Carers are responsible for:

*   Ensuring that their child attends school regularly and punctually unless prevented from doing so by illness or attendance at a medical appointment.
*   Contacting the school office on the first morning of absence.
*   Informing the school in advance of any medical appointments during school time.
*   Making requests for authorised absence in term time only if necessary, as these are not automatically authorised.
*   Talking to the school as soon as possible about any child’s reluctance to come to school so that problems can be quickly identified and dealt with.

**i. Registration**

The garden gate is open from 8:45 AM to 9:00 AM. During this time, all children are expected to come into their classroom.

Each class teacher has the responsibility for keeping an accurate record of attendance. Any pupil who is absent must be recorded at the beginning of the morning session.

All attendance records are documented using our systems, which the Head Teacher checks termly. Lateness and absences will be communicated termly via the student report to families.

**j. Lateness**

Once the doors are closed at 9:00 AM, the only way to get into school is via the main entrance. Any pupil who comes into school this way from 9:00 AM will be marked as late in the system and also in the late book. Records are kept of pupils who are late; this is documented on the digital register for each pupil.

Children who have attended a dentist or doctor’s appointment and subsequently come to school later than 9:00 AM will have the lateness recorded as medical.

Children who are persistently late miss a significant amount of learning, often the most important aspect, as the beginning of the day is where the teacher explains the plan and special events of the day.

Where there have been persistent incidents of lateness (more than 12 a term for IC and CH, and 6 for Primary), parents/carers will receive a letter advising them of the concerns, and the school will provide opportunities for parents/carers to seek support and advice to address these issues.

**k. Absences**

Parents/carers should contact the school on the first day of their child’s absence. When parents/carers notify us of their child’s absence, it is important that they provide us with details of the reason for their absence.

All absences are recorded as either authorised or unauthorised absences on the system. It is important that we receive accurate information from parents with reasons for the child’s absence. This information is used to determine whether the absence is authorised or unauthorised. The teacher has the responsibility to determine whether absences are authorised or unauthorised.

Where we have not received reasons for a child’s absence, the secretary will call to request these details.

**l. First Day Contact**

Where a child is absent from school and we have not received any verbal or written communication from the parent, then we initiate a first-day contact process. The secretary checks all the registers from 9:00 AM to 9:30 AM on a daily basis to identify those pupils who are absent. There are occasions when we are unaware why the child is absent, and we will contact the parent to check the reasons for the child’s absence.

**m. Addressing Attendance Concerns**

The school expects attendance of at least 80% for CH and 90% for Primary and 95% for Secondary.

It is important for children to establish good attendance habits early on in their primary school career. It is the responsibility of the coordinator to support good attendance and to identify and address attendance concerns promptly. In primary school, we rely upon parents to ensure their child attends school regularly and punctually. Therefore, where there are concerns regarding attendance, parents are always informed of our concerns. Initially, concerns about attendance are raised with parents via letters which are sent home. There will be opportunities for the parent/carer to discuss reasons for absence and for support to be given by the school with the aim to improve attendance.

## **3.5. Monitoring and Review** {#monitoring-and-review}

**Monitoring Attendance**:

Our secretary has the responsibility for ensuring that all attendance data is accurately recorded on Phidias and Transparent Classroom software. Regular meetings are held with the coordinator to discuss all attendance concerns, and appropriate actions are taken following these meetings, such as letters sent to parents or meetings arranged to discuss attendance concerns with parents.

The implementation and effectiveness of this policy will be reviewed annually by the school leadership team. Key performance indicators, such as attendance rates, punctuality trends, and feedback from parents and staff, will inform the review process. Necessary amendments will be made to ensure alignment with current regulations and the evolving needs of the school community.

The Head Teacher will oversee the monitoring of attendance trends termly and provide a summary report to the school board. Persistent or significant concerns will be addressed promptly with relevant families and staff members.

# **4. Behaviour Policy** {#behaviour-policy}

## **4.1. Scope** {#scope-1}

This policy applies to all members of the Imagine Montessori School community, including students, staff, and parents. It outlines expectations for behaviour, strategies for fostering a positive environment, and approaches for addressing behavioural concerns. The policy is designed to support children in their personal, social, and emotional development while fostering a sense of community and collaboration.

It is applicable in all settings within the school, including classrooms, playgrounds, and any school-related activities or events, whether onsite or offsite.

## **4.2. Terminology** {#terminology-2}

*   **Positive Behaviour Approach**: A strategy focusing on reinforcing desirable behaviours and guiding children in making appropriate choices.
*   **Natural Consequences**: Outcomes that naturally follow a child’s behaviour without adult-imposed punishments, allowing them to learn responsibility.
*   **Behaviour Flowchart**: A step-by-step guide for staff to manage behavioural incidents in a consistent and scalable manner.
*   **Peace Table**: A designated space where children can resolve conflicts through dialogue and mutual understanding.
*   **Student Support Team (SST)**: A team comprising the Head Teacher and the school psychologist to provide tailored support for behavioural and emotional development.
*   **Classroom Reflection**: A form used to document behavioural incidents and track patterns for minor, major, or unacceptable behaviours.

## **4.3. Principles and Statements** {#principles-and-statements}

At Imagine Montessori, we respect each child as an individual and their capacity to integrate socially with others. To build on this aspect of community and collaboration, we have certain expectations of appropriate behaviour to build on the happiness and security of every child.

Expectations of behaviour are clearly stated for the well-being of all, and to encourage the emotional support of all, we have instituted a Positive Behaviour approach. This includes the right for everyone to have a safe, positive, and happy experience at school.

Without exception, bullying is not tolerated at Imagine, and we have a separate, specific anti-bullying policy to safeguard against this behaviour.

## **4.4. Methods** {#methods}

Every staff member at Imagine has a pre-designated role to act as an appropriate role model with the use of positive language and behaviour. Children and parents also contribute to these standards with varying degrees of responsibility.

We expect all staff members to:

*   Be respectful to each other and use a positive behaviour approach.
*   Be a good role model to other adults and children in their care.
*   Be engaged in the activities so that the children can use their time with purpose and to their full potential.
*   Provide emotional support to all children in their care to help them meet the behavioural expectations.
*   Work in conjunction with children and parents to aid in the development of the child’s positive behaviour.
*   Work as a team to respond to all children's needs: academical, emotional, social, developmental, etc.

## **4.5. Responsibility for Behaviour** {#responsibility-for-behaviour}

The Head Teachers have overall responsibility for behaviour at Imagine Montessori, which includes personal, social, and emotional development and any related issues arising within this framework.

The Head Teachers will strive to:

*   Keep up-to-date on any new legislation arising, including research in positive behaviour and instituting a Student Support Team, which will include the Head Teacher and school psychologist.
*   Keep staff up-to-date and offer professional development where required to assist them in the function of their roles and responsibilities within the school.

## **4.6. Principles of Behaviour Policy** {#principles-of-behaviour-policy}

*   Grace and courtesy are our key principles as per Maria Montessori’s philosophy.
*   We respect all cultures and differences arising therein.
*   Every staff member at Imagine Montessori School will serve as a positive role model for behaviour.
*   All new staff will have an induction, which will include familiarizing them with the policy and the expectations.
*   We expect all members of our community to treat others with the respect and care afforded to every individual to make them feel included in our community and school ethos.
*   Staff and parents work in partnership to assure that positive behavioural supports are in place for all children in our care, and parents are informed regularly about their child’s behaviour.
*   Parents are also expected to inform the school of any changes in behaviour or family circumstances.
*   The Behaviour Flowchart guides our actions in deciding jointly how to respond appropriately in the event of instances that require our support.
*   We value individuality and understand different children may have different needs.

## **4.7. Appropriate Behaviour** {#appropriate-behaviour}

Good behaviour should be acknowledged and respected. Even in a Montessori environment, we can thank others for being kind or demonstrating grace or courtesy in situations. This might be through guides in the class or at a peer level.

“Thank you for walking so nicely…”
“Thank you for coming in so quietly…”
“Thank you for waiting…”

This will vary according to the needs of the individuals involved. This positive comment avoids the idea of extrinsic motivation and focuses on recognition of the individual when necessary to help maintain standards.

Standards of behaviour must be made explicit and described in behavioural terms that are relevant to them:

What does respect look like in the playground?

*   Asking if you can take turns or share the equipment.
*   Respecting the space of others and their games.
*   Being kind and including everyone in games.

## **4.8. When Behaviour Falls Short of Expectations** {#when-behaviour-falls-short-of-expectations}

When there is a minimum expectation (e.g., common courtesy), staff might gently remind a child with a question: “How do you think X felt when you said that?” “Was that a kind thing to do to your friend? How could we fix it?”

If we need to remind children of the core values: Respect, Caring, be Responsible, Calm attitude, we use the key words to restate expectations.

## **4.9. Dealing with Inappropriate Behaviours** {#dealing-with-inappropriate-behaviours}

As a team, we strive to work from a preventative perspective rather than reacting. When we intervene at an early stage, we can provide children with tools and strategies at a moment when they can learn them better. However, there are always times when we cannot stop inappropriate behaviours from happening.

In dealing with any inappropriate behaviours, our first task is to ensure that the child can understand why that behaviour was inappropriate and take responsibility.

As a staff, we treat all children with respect, and physical interventions are not used except in extreme cases where the safety of others is at stake.

We ask children to describe what happened, explain the impact of their action (if they are unable to do this), and understand why this is inappropriate. This discussion may happen immediately or after, depending on the child.

Our behaviour flowchart (see image below) is used to guide actions and interventions in a scalable order of minor and major incidents. A behaviour referral form is used to track behavioural incidents for minor, major, or unacceptable concerns. This will be designated by the person who witnesses the incident/behaviour. Each time a child fills in an incident form, this will be sent home via email to inform families. After 5 minor incidents or 3 major incidents occur, the school will contact the family to pick up the child, and they will have to remain home the following day. It is very important to keep parents informed and work together to offer the child help at both school and home. The Student Support Team is involved at this stage, and next steps for the parents will be agreed upon.

From a Positive Behaviour Support approach, it is recognised that reasons for inappropriate behaviours might require immediate action, including removing the child from the situation until a resolution can be calmly sought. This should be done especially if a child presents a danger to themselves or others. This should not be seen as a sanction nor treated as one. Children will never be left on their own in these occasions.

## **4.10. Natural Consequences** {#natural-consequences}

At Imagine Montessori School, we avoid extrinsic motivation, including sticker charts and point systems. We do acknowledge when children make good choices. Natural consequences that occur in line with the behaviour flowchart follow the choices that the children make, and we work towards making them cognisant of taking responsibility for their actions.

## **4.11. Day to Day Strategies** {#day-to-day-strategies}

To manage and maintain behaviour daily, we have adopted the following:

*   Positive Language used in day-to-day interactions between staff and children.
*   Modelling appropriate behaviour and responding to it, paying special attention to when it comes from children.
*   Circle and group time: to be able to discuss issues arising, teach and practise strategies, etc.
*   Reinstating relationships: when children are involved in an issue, it isn't enough to just “fix” the problem; we work towards giving opportunities to restore the relationship to a cordial and friendly one again.
*   Behaviour Flow Chart: outlines natural consequences ensuing from behaviours of a minor or major nature.
*   Classroom Reflection: used to track instances of a major incident.
*   Positive Behaviour Supports in place, including our Student Support Team, to help with facilitating communication between home and meetings between psychologist and staff, children, and parents to help with next steps and strategies.

We use a 3-tier system which includes the following:

*   At Tier 1, after reminders from teachers, the child can refocus and adapt behaviour to meet expectations.
*   At Tier 2, the behaviour flow chart is followed, and a classroom reflection might be used to track behaviours. After 3 to 5 incidents, parents are called to discuss how to support their child.
*   At Tier 3, the child has a repetitive major behavioural issue, and an individual support plan is required to be put in place and possibly a one-to-one to support throughout the day.

We always rely on prevention rather than intervention, so staff are adequately supported with strategies to prevent children from moving to Tier 2 and Tier 3.

## **4.12. Special Intervention Support: Tier 3** {#special-intervention-support:-tier-3}

If a child is on Tier 3, where they require specific support to manage their behaviour, a one-to-one support will usually be employed to support during the work cycle and any other aspects of the day, based on the specific needs of the child.

A meeting will be held with the parents to explain the need, the intention, and the strategies the support will provide. Imagine Montessori School, being a private educational institution, cannot provide this support as an internal resource. Therefore, it is critical that parents are involved from the beginning to understand why they need to provide this support for their child. A weekly summary will be provided to the parents by the one-to-one support and monitored by the SST.

The Student Support Team, consisting of the Head Teacher and the psychologist, will meet regularly with the one-to-one support and the teacher to discuss next steps with the positive behaviour intervention.

The individual support plan for the child will outline the strategies to be implemented for learning growth and development. The weekly 1-1 summary will be matched against this plan to track progress made.

## **4.13. Removal from the Class** {#removal-from-the-class}

Following the behaviour flowchart, if a child needs to be removed from the classroom because they present a danger to themselves or others, OR normal reminders are not working and they need to take a break, this should not be presented as a sanction but a normal flow of consequences resulting from their choices.

A choice is always presented to the child before removal from a class, situation, or location. He/she can choose to modify their behaviour to meet the expectations of the situation or can leave the place to take a break. When they are ready, they may come back into the situation, with a clear idea of what their next steps will be.

If a child has a special intervention plan, they may have this included as a strategy to help them self-regulate their behaviour.

## **4.14. Immediate Action** {#immediate-action}

Staff have the right to impose immediate action with children displaying inappropriate behaviour. These are always relevant to the particular child and involve the removal of short-term privileges or natural consequences established in the classroom by the children with the support of the guide. Examples of such actions might include:

*   Working on their own rather than with peers.
*   Loss of privilege (e.g., park-time, social lunch).
*   Restricted to work in a particular area.

A “classroom reflection” might or might not be filled in, depending on the severity of the situations.

## **4.15. Disagreements Between Pupils** {#disagreements-between-pupils}

Part of educating our children involves them learning social skills, including how to manage and resolve conflicts. We refrain from intervening as adults while staying close and monitoring how the children manage the situation and resolve their conflicts. As adults, we can offer them mediation and support towards resolving their problems (strategies).

To do this, we have daily circle time when issues can be brought up, and children are asked to comment on actions or behaviours they noticed in their peers. They are also asked to reflect on their own actions and behaviours. Examples could include:

*   Positive: “Today I felt good when I helped X with their work.”
*   Challenge: “I am working on helping to tidy up after an activity.”
*   Improve on: “I am working on being a kind friend in the park by sharing the materials.”
*   Enjoyed: “I really liked it today when X came to help me put my shoes on.”

They also have the option to resolve conflicts at the ‘Peace Table’. If a child feels they need to resolve something, they might ‘invite’ a friend to the peace table. The child who requested the visit explains why they invited their friend. The other child listens and then has a turn to speak. They stay at the Peace Table until the conflict is resolved. An option is to have a ‘turn to talk’ object they pass to each other to help them take turns speaking and listening. The idea generated is how to fix their problem rather than throw accusations at each other. It is a good idea for an adult to be close by to help mediate if necessary.

## **4.16. General Principles** {#general-principles}

The staff are role models when it comes to positive behaviour, demeanour, language, and actions. The following principles apply to all staff:

*   Every staff member at Imagine uses positive strategies to handle inappropriate behaviour and to help children find solutions that are age-appropriate.
*   The peace table is a dedicated forum for this, as well as circle time in a group setting. Discussions should always model positive language that can be used: “Today I felt sad when X didn’t share the playground toys with me,” versus an accusatory, “You didn’t let me play!” We should always support children to gain control of their emotions by taking a break if necessary before they try to resolve their problem (e.g., deep breathing exercises, walk-away).
*   The staff should be aware of triggers for children on Special Support Plans and always work towards prevention rather than intervention.

**WE DO:**

*   Reinforce positive behaviour by acknowledging it as part of grace and courtesy, and we teach children to do the same.
*   Check and reflect on our attitude and language to ensure we are positive role models.
*   Support each other and each child in developing confidence and feelings of competence.
*   Avoid creating situations where children receive adult attention only in response to inappropriate behaviours.
*   Help children to develop a sense of empathy and to recognise the consequences of their choices or actions.

**WE DON'T:**

*   Never send children out to stand in a corridor.
*   Make them feel sanctions are being imposed as a negative consequence as opposed to a natural consequence.
*   Never use physical force with children or touch them in a rough manner.
*   Never compare a child.
*   Never use techniques to single out and humiliate a child.
*   Do not shout or raise voice in a threatening way in response to inappropriate behaviour.

## **4.17. Physical Restraint** {#physical-restraint}

*   In special circumstances, we may use physical restraint such as holding or picking up a child. We ONLY ever do this to prevent physical injury to themselves, other children/adults, or damage to property. When it is practically possible, we let a child know what we are going to do beforehand.
*   Details of a serious incident of this nature are required to be catalogued. We use the “classroom reflection” tracker for this and describe in detail what happened in the ‘note’ section of the form. Details include what happened, who was there, what action was taken and by whom. Details of the incident are kept in the child's personal file. The parent is informed on the same day as per the requirements of the referral tracking form.

## **4.18. Inappropriate Language** {#inappropriate-language}

Imagine Montessori School is inclusive, and any behaviour that can be categorised as abusive, including negative stereotypes or discrimination of any type, is not appropriate. We immediately make clear the unacceptability of any of these behaviours or attitudes. We do this by explaining what is being done, the harm it causes, and why it is not appropriate. We do not personally blame nor create more stereotypes by labelling individuals as bullies or racists.

## **4.19. Rough Play That Involves Aggression** {#rough-play-that-involves-aggression}

Children can often engage in play that can be based on aggressive themes: superhero and weapon play. Children who engage in this type of play are not necessarily trying to be hurtful. If this play becomes inconsiderate, we need to address it with the following strategies:

*   We understand that this type of play is normal within boundaries and that children like to engage in imaginative play of ‘goodies’ and ‘baddies’. Unless this play becomes hurtful, we do not interfere.
*   We agree ground rules with these children about fantasy play and hurting someone.
*   Encourage children to say no: “Stop, I don’t like it.”
*   We encourage children to learn to accept when someone says “no” and stop.
*   We observe and understand this play and can offer more appropriate alternatives when we see it getting excessively rough.

## **4.20. Younger Children and Hurtful Behaviour** {#younger-children-and-hurtful-behaviour}

Sometimes young children say hurtful things impulsively without considering what they are saying or the consequences. We use these as teachable moments and help them to manage their feelings in these moments or later, once they are calm. We help young children to learn to empathise through a variety of strategies including role play, modelling behaviour, and circle time. They also learn that they need to fix the problem, instead of just saying sorry.

When these behaviours become problematic, we involve parents to identify the cause and find a solution together. The main reasons for excessive hurtful behaviour usually include:

*   They do not feel securely attached to someone who can interpret and meet their needs: this may be at home or in the school setting.
*   Their parent or carer in the school setting does not have the requisite skills to respond appropriately, and consequently, negative patterns start emerging.
*   The child is exposed to aggressive behaviour at home or from peers at school.
*   In the case of suspected child abuse, we are required to report it to Social Services.
*   The child has a developmental condition that affects how they behave.

In meetings with the school psychologist, we will aim to provide a unified approach in dealing with these behaviours, both at home and in school.

## **4.21. Monitoring and Review** {#4.21.-monitoring-and-review}

The Behaviour Policy will be monitored and reviewed annually by the Leadership Team and the Student Support Team. Feedback from staff, parents, and students will be incorporated to ensure the policy remains effective and relevant. The review process will consider:

1.  Behavioural incident records and trends.
2.  Feedback from staff and families.
3.  The effectiveness of strategies and interventions.
4.  Alignment with new research or legislation related to behaviour management.

Updates will be communicated to the school community, and training will be provided to ensure consistent implementation.

# **5. Anti-Racism Policy** {#anti---racism-policy}

## **5.1. Scope** {#scope-2}

This policy applies to all members of the Imagine Montessori School community, including students, staff, parents, carers, and visitors. It covers all school activities, including lessons, assemblies, extracurricular activities, and off-site trips. The policy addresses both direct and indirect racism, whether it occurs on school premises, during school-related activities, or in any interaction involving the school community.

## **5.2. Terminology** {#terminology-3}

*   **Racism**: A form of bullying or discrimination based on an individual’s colour, ethnicity, culture, or geographical origin. This includes both institutional and personal racism.
*   **Personal Racism**: Direct and identifiable acts of harassment or discrimination, such as verbal abuse, physical assault, or racist graffiti.
*   **Equality**: The principle of ensuring equal opportunities and fair treatment for all, regardless of race or ethnicity.
*   **Diversity**: The recognition and celebration of differences among individuals, including cultural and racial differences.

## **5.3. Policy Statement and Principles** {#policy-statement-and-principles-2}

Imagine Montessori School is a multi-ethnic community with a significant international student body. It is essential we take steps to educate our community about issues related to racism while highlighting the positive cultural value of a diverse ethnic mix from a global perspective.

Imagine Montessori School will be proactive in implementing its duties and will seek to promote racial equality and good race relations, and to eliminate racial discrimination. Imagine Montessori School is committed to identifying and removing discriminatory practices and any form of racism or racist behaviour.

## **5.4. Aims of the School Anti-Racism Policy** {#aims-of-the-school-anti-racism-policy}

The school aims to promote, in the widest sense, a happy and nurturing environment in which individuals are appreciated and respected. All pupils and staff are expected to further this objective. Racism is addressed across the curriculum, for example, in PSHE, Religious Studies, Assemblies, and Circle time.

Imagine Montessori School aims to promote race equality and actively tackle racial discrimination within all areas of school life:

*   To challenge racial discrimination, racist behaviour, racist language, or harassment.
*   Prejudice and stereotyping, however thoughtless or unintentional.
*   To make sure that all students and staff are encouraged and supported to achieve their full potential.
*   To provide an environment which respects and values diversity and shows consideration for the traditions, cultures, and religious practices of people from different racial groups and different geographical regions.
*   To prevent direct and indirect, overt and covert discrimination on grounds of race or geographical origin.
*   To assist in the identification of possible barriers to equality of opportunity for students and staff and to ensure that these barriers are addressed where possible.

## **5.5. Definition of Racism** {#definition-of-racism}

Racism is a form of bullying. It can be both (a) institutional and (b) personal, overt or subtle, intentional or unintentional.

**a. Institutional Racism**

The Stephen Lawrence Inquiry Report defined this as: ‘The collective failure of an organisation to provide an appropriate and professional service to people because of their colour, culture, or ethnic origin. It can be seen or detected in processes, attitudes, and behaviour which amount to discrimination through unwitting prejudice, ignorance, thoughtlessness, and racist stereotyping which disadvantages minority ethnic people.’

When a child is subject to racist bullying or harassment, their behaviour and attainment are likely to be affected. If the behaviour is treated in isolation without taking into consideration the issues and effects of racism, this can be described as institutional racism. The racist element must be explicitly recognised and dealt with.

This type of racism is also covert and indirect and therefore can be difficult to identify and address. It is often evident in the assumptions, beliefs, and values that affect people’s instinctive responses. It can be demonstrated subconsciously in subtle ways, and Imagine Montessori School recognises the need consciously to challenge such attitudes.

**b. Personal Racism**

This is often direct and identifiable and can be manifested through harassment and offensive behaviour in the following ways:

*   Physical assault against a person or group due to their colour, ethnicity, geographical origin, or culture.
*   Derogatory name-calling, insults, ridiculing, and racist jokes.
*   Racist graffiti.
*   Provocative behaviour such as wearing racist badges or insignia.
*   Verbal abuse and threats.
*   Incitement of others to behave in a racist way.
*   Racist comments within the context of lessons.

## **5.6. Accountability and Responsibility** {#accountability-and-responsibility}

The Senior Leadership Team is responsible for ensuring that the school meets its commitments under its Anti-Racism Policy. In addition, they will provide any necessary help to staff to keep up-to-date with any changes in race relations legislation.

*   The Headteachers will ensure that all staff are aware of their responsibilities and are given the support to exercise this responsibility.
*   All racist incidents that are reported will need to be investigated and recorded.
*   All staff are required to promote racial equality and good race relations.
*   Staff are expected not to discriminate on racial grounds and are expected to attend training or to read information provided by the Headteachers about any change to policy.
*   Staff are expected to always challenge racist and geographically biased attitudes and behaviour.
*   Staff are also expected to be aware of the need to report suspicions of racism to the Headteacher.
*   Where appropriate, the school will be prepared to contact the police if a racial crime has been committed.

## **5.7. Action by the School When Racism is Suspected or Reported** {#action-by-the-school-when-racism-is-suspected-or-reported}

Alleged incidents of racism should be investigated in line with our Behaviour Policy. We have Incident Report Forms (school drive) which can be used to help systematically record incident details as the first part of an investigation.

Possible disciplinary action by the school:

*   An official warning from the Headteacher.
*   Exclusion from certain areas of school premises and life (such as co-curricular activities).
*   In serious cases or where there is repeated racism, exclusion (temporary) from the school for a fixed term.
*   Continued racist behaviour will be incompatible with staying at the school, and permanent exclusion may be used in such cases.

It is important that the victim of the racist incident is satisfied with the outcome of any disciplinary measures taken. It is also important to remember that anyone who has been a victim of a racist incident has the legal right to report it as a crime to the police.

See the school’s Anti-Bullying Policy for further details about our response to bullying in general.

**Implementing and Reviewing the Anti-Racism Policy**

New staff will be introduced to the policy during their induction.

There will be a formal review of the policy every year, and any necessary amendments will be circulated to staff and families.

The Anti-Racism Policy will be reviewed annually by the Leadership Team to ensure its effectiveness and alignment with current legislation and best practices. Input from staff, students, and parents will be sought to ensure the policy reflects the needs of the community. Any updates or changes will be communicated promptly to all stakeholders.

# **6. Anti-Bullying Policy** {#anti---bullying-policy}

## **6.1. Scope** {#scope-3}

This policy applies to all members of the Imagine Montessori School community, including students, staff, parents, and visitors. It is relevant to all activities occurring within the school grounds, during school-sponsored events, and in any interactions involving members of the school community, including online or remote communications.

## **6.2. Terminology** {#terminology-common-to-policies}

*   **Bullying**: Deliberate, repeated, and continuous hurtful behaviour aimed at causing distress to another individual. This includes physical, verbal, psychological, and cyber forms.
*   **Victim**: The individual subjected to bullying behaviour.
*   **Perpetrator**: The individual or group engaging in bullying behaviour.
*   **Cyber-bullying**: Bullying through digital means such as text messages, social media, or emails.
*   **PSHEE**: Personal, Social, Health, and Economic Education.

## **6.3. Policy Statement and Principles** {#policy-statement-and-principles-3}

At Imagine Montessori School, we strongly believe that children have a right to study in a safe, supportive, and secure environment. Incidents of bullying threaten this and cause enormous stress to victims. We are therefore strongly committed to the avoidance of bullying in all its forms.

We believe that the principal means of preventing it is through the maintenance of conditions where bullying is less likely to flourish and is more easily detected. At Imagine Montessori School, all pupils will be known to us personally, and it will therefore be easier for staff to detect signs of possible distress. Moreover, we feel that it will be important that pupils have free and informal access to the Head and other staff. A formal anti-bullying policy will be of utmost importance.

As part of our Anti-Bullying Policy, Imagine Montessori School believes that all children and adults have the right to live in a supportive, caring environment in which pupils feel safe and free from bullying and harassment that may include cyber-bullying and prejudice-based bullying related to special educational needs, sexual orientation, sex, race, religion and belief, gender reassignment, or disability, and the use of discriminatory language. Bullying can occur through several types of anti-social behaviour.

## **6.4. Policy Objectives** {#policy-objectives}

*   To enable all members of the school community to understand what constitutes bullying, making it clear to children, staff, and families that bullying is completely unacceptable and that children and young people have the right to be protected from physical, written, and oral abuse or intimidation.
*   To prevent bullying by raising awareness with all members of the school community, ensuring they have a clear understanding of what bullying behaviour is.
*   To ensure that all members of the school community know and understand the school policy on bullying and follow the established procedures when bullying is suspected or reported.
*   To ensure that all pupils are confident to report bullying if they feel they are being bullied or they witness bullying towards another student.
*   To involve students actively in developing the anti-bullying ethos in the school.
*   To create and maintain a caring, kind, non-violent, and non-threatening atmosphere in the school and raise the school community awareness through educational opportunities that arise, such as PSHE and citizenship activities, circle time, and assemblies.
*   Students are entitled:
    *   To feel welcome and safe and not be made to feel unhappy by others.
    *   Not to be frightened by others.
    *   Not to be touched in an inappropriate manner.
    *   Not to be teased about birth, gender, age, race/ethnicity, sexual orientation, opinion, mental capacity, religion and belief, education, economic status, personality, or any other personal or social circumstance or condition.
    *   Not to be made unhappy by unpleasant comments, notes, letters, text messages, e-mails, phone calls, any form of gossip, or by any other means.
    *   To feel confident to report any concerns.

## **6.5. Rationale** {#rationale}

All members of the school have an entitlement to be in an educational environment that is safe, positive, secure, and supportive. Bullying interferes with this.

Bullying can be defined as a deliberate act done to cause distress solely to give a feeling of power, status, or other gratification to the bully. Bullying can range from ostracising, name-calling, teasing, threats, threatening or aggressive language or body posturing, and extortion, through physical intimidation, assault on persons and/or their property. It can be an unresolved single frightening incident that casts a shadow over a child's life, or a series of such incidents.

Staff, parents, and children work together to create a happy, caring, and safe learning environment. Bullying, whether verbal, physical, or indirect, is not tolerated. It is everyone's responsibility to try to prevent occurrences of bullying and to deal with any incidents quickly and effectively.

The victim(s), their friend(s), their parent(s), or other interested people may bring bullying to the attention of any member of staff.

## **6.6. Aims** {#aims}

*   To provide a safe, caring environment for the whole school community, especially the children in our care.
*   To promote in everyone a positive attitude and high self-esteem.
*   To help individuals to understand and accept the consequences of their own actions.
*   To develop a programme whereby all incidents of bullying can be reported in a secure atmosphere and will be dealt with fairly and appropriately.
*   To help individuals celebrate and value their differences and to acknowledge and respect the differences of others.
*   To instil in all members of the school community that bullying is unacceptable and that reports of bullying will be taken seriously, recorded, and acted upon.

## **6.7. Implementation** {#implementation}

**a. Framework for Preventing Bullying**

Prevention is better than cure, and Imagine Montessori tries to ensure that pupils and adults are educated in anti-bullying strategies. These include:

*   Ensuring that staff always listen to and act on complaints of bullying.
*   Encouraging pupils to tell a trusted adult if they feel threatened in any way.
*   Encouraging the caring and nurturing side of children through explicit teaching of PSHE/Citizenship combined with the Montessori moral awareness which has been developing within the school from when they started in the Infant Community (Pre-Nursery).
*   Working within the school through the curriculum to develop a caring, cooperative, responsible ethos.
*   Providing opportunities to discuss friendships and inter-personal skills, and using drama and role play to meet situations.
*   Ensuring adequate supervision in the playground.
*   Actively discouraging bullying.

## **6.8. Definition of Bullying** {#definition-of-bullying}

Bullying is behaviour which consists of physical, verbal, or psychological aggression from a student or students towards another student and/or these students place themselves in a position of superiority. This behaviour is repeated, continued over time, and deliberate.

Bullying can take many forms but have these elements in common:

*   Hurtful behaviour which is deliberate.
*   Hurtful behaviour which is repeated.
*   Hurtful behaviour which is continuous in time.
*   Hurtful behaviour which can take the form of physical, verbal, or psychological aggression.
*   Hurtful behaviour from which it is difficult to defend oneself; the bully places himself/herself in a position of superiority.

It can take the form of name-calling, threatened violence, actual violence, intimidation, isolation, ridicule, or other action such as spreading unpleasant stories and gossip, sending hurtful or intimidating text messages, e-mails, comments on social media, and telephone calls.

Communications technology (texting, e-mails, messaging, social network sites, Bluetooth) has also enabled a remote, potentially anonymous channel for bullying, popularly referred to as “Cyber-bullying”.

All bullying is designed to make the victim feel threatened, humiliated, and unsafe. Not all negative interactions between students can be deemed as bullying, and the school works hard to ensure that students understand the difference between bullying and ‘falling out’ or arguing.

All members of the School Community are expected to treat all potential bullying cases with utmost confidentiality, ensuring that all children’s rights are protected.

## **6.9. Preventing Bullying** {#preventing-bullying}

The school is committed to the ongoing development of an anti-bullying ethos throughout the school with students, staff, and parents.

Imagine Montessori School will use the following methods for helping children to prevent bullying. As and when appropriate, these may include:

*   The school rules through circle time, during Personal, Social, Health Economic (PSHEE) and citizenship activities.
*   Social stories and drawings about positive behaviour and role modelling.
*   Reading stories about positive behaviour expectations and bullying, having them read to a class.
*   Making up role-plays.
*   Providing emotional support to give each child the opportunity to express how they feel.

**Methods**:

*   We will watch for early signs of distress in children.
*   We will listen, believe, and act when a child(ren) communicates that they are being bullied.
*   We will intervene to stop the child who is bullying from harming the other child or children.
*   We will explain to the child doing the bullying why his or her behaviour is unacceptable.
*   We will give reassurance to the child or children who have been bullied.
*   We will help the child who has done the bullying to recognise the impact of his or her actions.
*   We support the child who has done the bullying to learn alternate strategies to manage their behaviour.

**Good Practice for Staff**:

*   Be continually aware, watchful, and available.
*   Promote good behaviour and encourage the care of others.
*   Ensure children are appropriately supervised.
*   Report all cases of bullying to the Head.
*   Parents’ involvement and cooperation can be sought, and parents of children involved will be kept informed.

## **6.10. Identifying and Reporting Bullying** {#identifying-and-reporting-bullying}

Students and families should report any suspicion of bullying immediately to any of the designated people below:

*   Tutor
*   Key Stage Manager
*   Head teacher

Much of the behaviour which leads to both bullying and intimidation is difficult to observe or can be seen as innocent when it is not. It is therefore essential that staff listen carefully to what children say and watch for any signs that a child may be being intimidated. The staff must always pass on their concern to the other members of staff working with the child and report these concerns to the Head Teacher.

**If you witness any unacceptable behaviour, how you should act:**

1.  **If you feel you are being bullied.**
    Tell someone.
    If you feel you are being bullied, you can tell a member of staff and/or your parent/carer (anyone you feel safe to talk to). It is not weak to do this.
    If you cannot tell anyone at school, you can tell your parents.
    Friends of those who feel bullied should let staff know of bullying incidents.
    No one deserves to be bullied. You have a right for this not to happen to you.

2.  **If you are a pupil and witness bullying behaviour.**
    Support the child who you observed being bullied by offering your friendship and making it clear that, in your opinion, what is happening to them is unkind.
    Encourage them to speak out on their own behalf by confronting the child who was exhibiting the bullying behaviour, or with their permission, confront that child yourself. Accompany the child who you observed being bullied to a trusted adult or suggest that you speak to an adult on their behalf.

3.  **If you are a member of Staff.**
    Reassure and support the pupils involved.
    Advise them that you are required to pass details on to their Guide and/or to the named person responsible for supporting good behaviour (Key Stage Coordinator/Head Teacher).
    The named person will keep a log of all complaints or incidences of bullying and record the way in which they were dealt with.

4.  **If you are a Parent.**
    Bring the issue to the attention of any member of staff, or to the Head of School, or the named person responsible for supporting good behaviour.

## **6.11. Taking Action** {#taking-action}

**Interview the child who has been bullied.**

The child who has experienced the bullying will be interviewed by their Guide, who may ask them to write an immediate account of events. The Guide may also invite the child to discuss their own reaction and behaviour towards the child who bullied them. The process for dealing with the incident will be clearly explained to them. The Guide may give them further support and advice if deemed appropriate.

**Interview the child responsible for that behaviour.**

Once the Guide is clear that a bullying incident has occurred, the child who was responsible for the bullying will be interviewed, as will any others related to the incident. Each will be asked to write an immediate account of events. The process for dealing with the incident will be clearly explained to them.

**Actions and Consequences**

The following sanctions may be applied in accordance with the school’s Behaviour Policy:

1.  **Formal School Warning - Incident Report - Families Informed.**
    A formal school warning is a written or verbal notice given to a student who has been involved in bullying. It serves as an official acknowledgment of the behaviour and a warning that further actions will be taken if the behaviour continues.

    *   **Process**:
        *   **Investigation**: Before issuing a formal warning, the adult who witnesses the incident, the Guide, and the Head Teacher will investigate the bullying incident to gather facts, speak with witnesses, and understand the context.
        *   **Families Informed**: Clear and timely communication with the families of both the bully and the victim, ensuring they are aware of the situation, the actions taken by the school, and the support available. The school contacts the families as soon as possible after the incident has been reported and investigated. This is typically done via phone call, followed by a written notice.
        *   The school may arrange meetings with the families to discuss the incident, share the findings of the investigation, and explain the actions taken by the school. For the family of the bully, this may include discussing the formal warning and any further consequences. For the family of the victim, the focus will be on the support and protection measures in place for their child.
        *   The school maintains ongoing communication with the families to keep them informed of any developments. This includes updates on how the situation is being monitored and any further incidents or resolutions.
        *   **Warning Issued**: The school issues the formal warning, which may include a written notice that outlines the incident, suspension, or exclusion following the bullying policy. The warning is documented in the student's record.
        *   **Expectations and Consequences**: The student is informed of the expectations for their future behaviour and the consequences they will face if they do not adhere to these expectations. This might include suspension or exclusion if the behaviour continues.

2.  **Suspension.**
    *   **Definition**: Suspension is a temporary removal of a student from their regular school environment due to misbehaviour or violation of school policies.
    *   **Duration**: Suspensions can vary from a day to a few days or weeks, depending on the severity of the behaviour. The student is expected to return to school after the suspension period ends.
    *   **Purpose**: The main goal of suspension is to provide a consequence for the student's actions while also giving them time to reflect on their behaviour. It also serves as a warning to the student and others that certain behaviours are unacceptable.
    *   **Types**:
        *   In-School Suspension: The student remains at school but is isolated from their peers and regular activities.
        *   Out-of-School Suspension: The student is not allowed to attend school or participate in any school-related activities during the suspension period.

3.  **Exclusion.**
    *   **Definition**: Exclusion, also known as expulsion or permanent exclusion, is the permanent removal of a student from the school.
    *   **Duration**: Exclusion is indefinite and means that the student is no longer allowed to attend the school from which they were excluded. The student would need to seek education elsewhere.
    *   **Purpose**: Exclusion is used as a last resort when a student's behaviour is so severe or repeated that the school believes they can no longer be a part of the school community. This might be due to actions that pose a significant risk to the safety or well-being of others or a repeated failure to adhere to school rules despite previous interventions.
    *   **Process**: Exclusion usually involves a formal process, including meetings with the student, parents or guardians, and school administrators. There may be an appeal process where the decision can be reviewed.

This may be reported to the local authority.

These are minimum sanctions. In very serious cases, it may be necessary to make a report to the Police or Social Services. However, it is the policy of the school to attempt to resolve such issues internally using our own disciplinary sanctions, unless the matter is of such gravity that a criminal prosecution is likely.

The staff will discuss this policy and its implications on their practice at least once every year. Where possible, parents will be invited to contribute to the school’s policy and procedures to prevent bullying. The records of bullying offences will be reviewed by the Management Team once a year at their ‘Dashboard Meeting’ at the end of each school year.

In parallel, we will tackle the issue within the year group/class.

Each incident will be discussed at the weekly staff meeting to inform the other members of staff about how it has been addressed and prevent further incidents.

## **7.2. Terminology** {#terminology-4}

## **7.3. Statutory Duties** {#statutory-duties}

Schools have a statutory duty to promote pupils’ wellbeing and hence have a clear role in preventing drug misuse as part of their pastoral care. Although there is no statutory requirement to have a Drug Policy, it is the advice of the DfE for schools to have a clear procedure for managing any incidents related to drugs/tobacco or alcohol misuse. It is essential to protect staff, parent/carer(s), children, and young people.

Spanish legislation (Ley 28/2005 de 26 de diciembre, as modified by Ley 42/2010 de 30 de diciembre) prohibits smoking in and around educational establishments. The Statutory Framework for the Early Years Foundation Stage (2021) requires the following (para 3.57): ‘Providers must not allow smoking, vaping, or the use of e-cigarettes in or on the premises when children are present or about to be present.’ This includes offsite trips and visits. Furthermore, practitioners must not be under the influence of alcohol or any other substances which may affect their ability to care for children, as per their Code of Conduct.

# **8. Weapons and Dangerous Items Policy** {#8.-weapons-and-dangerous-items-policy}

## **8.1. Scope** {#8.1.-scope}

This policy applies to all students, staff, parents, visitors, and any other individuals accessing the Imagine Montessori School (IMS) site. It outlines the preventative measures, actions, and procedures in place to address the presence or use of weapons within the school grounds, as well as the support and safety measures provided to our community in such situations. This policy covers all areas of the school premises, during school hours, and at any school-organized events or activities, whether on or off-site.

## **8.2. Terminology** {#8.2.-terminology}

*   **Weapon**: For the purposes of this policy, a weapon includes but is not limited to:
    *   Firearms, including starting pistols, air guns, pellet guns, BB guns, and replicas or toy guns.
    *   Bladed objects such as knives, craft knives, scissors, and razor blades.
    *   Explosives, including fireworks.
    *   Other objects like aerosol sprays, lighters, matches, laser pens, and martial arts equipment (e.g., nunchaku, death stars).
    *   Tools such as screwdrivers, hammers, and chisels that can be offensively used.
*   **Prohibited Item**: Any object identified under this policy that poses a potential risk to the safety and well-being of the school community.
*   **Safety Plan**: A structured intervention created for a pupil following a weapon-related incident, aimed at ensuring their safety and the safety of others while promoting positive behaviour and support.

## **8.3. Policy Statement and Principles** {#8.3.-policy-statement-and-principles}

1.  **Commitment to Safety**: At IMS, the safety and well-being of all community members is a top priority. Immediate action will always be taken to reduce risks and ensure a secure environment for everyone on-site.
2.  **Preventative Culture**:
    *   IMS promotes a strong safeguarding culture where staff, students, and parents are vigilant and proactive in addressing potential risks.
    *   The Personal, Social, Health, and Economic (PSHE) curriculum educates students on the dangers and legal implications of carrying or using weapons.
    *   Open communication between the school and parents is essential to reinforce a shared responsibility in addressing safety concerns.
3.  **Responsiveness and Individual Context**:
    *   Every incident involving a weapon will be handled based on the individual circumstances, intent, and level of risk posed.
    *   Decisions will prioritize the safety of all while considering the needs of the pupil involved, with actions ranging from exclusion to safety planning and support.
4.  **Zero Tolerance**:
    *   Weapons of any kind are strictly prohibited on school premises.
    *   Staff will take immediate action upon suspicion or discovery of a weapon, including searches, confiscation, and necessary reporting to authorities.

## **8.4. Purpose and Aim** {#8.4.-purpose-and-aim}

At Imagine Montessori School, the purpose of this policy guidance is to clearly set out the preventative actions in place to prevent the use of any weapons on our site. This policy guidance will also show the direct actions a school will take around the use of weapons on site, taking into account the full context around each and every incident.

At IMS, we are committed to the safety and well-being of all of our community and will always take immediate action to reduce the risk of harm to any individual on our school site.

**What is a weapon?**

For the purpose of this Policy, a “weapon” is: a firearm of any description, including starting pistols, air guns, and any type of replica or toy gun, pellet guns, or BB guns; knives, including all variations of bladed objects, i.e., pocket knives, craft knives, scissors, etc.; explosives, including fireworks, aerosol sprays, lighters, matches; laser pens or other objects, even if manufactured for a non-violent purpose, but have a potentially violent use, i.e., the purpose of keeping or carrying the object is for use, or threat of use, as a weapon. Nunchucks, death stars, and other martial arts objects. Screwdrivers, hammers, chisels, and any tool that could be offensively used. Razors, razor blades, or chains.

**Preventative Strategies**

ALL staff must read and understand this policy and the expectations of keeping children safe. Creating a safeguarding culture so that pupils and staff can feel safe requires everyone to agree to and work within the school’s ethos and culture of vigilance and positively contribute to the sharing of information where there may be risk. By creating a strong culture of safeguarding within the school, all individuals accessing the site should feel safe to raise concerns, seek support, and for a seamless and consistent response to incidents to be followed. Effective use of the curriculum, having an effective curriculum in place via PSHE, should support pupils in understanding the risks around the use of weapons, the legal implications of carrying weapons, and the school’s response to such incidents. Parents need to be aware of the school’s stringent policy around weapons and support the decisions made by senior leaders in the event of an incident. Parents should always be vigilant of the behaviours of their own children and seek support and intervention when necessary.

## **8.5. Searching & Confiscation** {#8.5.-searching-&-confiscation}

1.  **Searching**
    School staff can search a pupil for any item if the pupil agrees. Headteachers and staff have a statutory power to search pupils or their possessions, without consent, where they have reasonable grounds for suspecting that the pupil may have a prohibited item.

2.  **Confiscating**
    School staff can seize any prohibited item found as a result of a search. They can also seize any item they consider harmful or detrimental to school discipline.

3.  **Deciding on the Next Course of Action**
    From the outcome of the investigation, the school must decide on the level of risk the pupil poses in school. If the risk is clear and the intent is obvious, then a fixed-term exclusion and a disciplinary hearing may follow, or the decision to permanently exclude may be made (following necessary protocols, reporting to the police, social services, etc.). However, depending on the intent, the age of the child, and also the circumstances surrounding the incident or the contextual issues, it may be that a fixed-term exclusion is given, followed by a risk assessment/safety plan put in place following the pupil’s return to school.

    **Returning to School/Safety Planning**
    Safety planning is a positive way of supporting a child who may benefit from a planned approach to support or intervention following an incident where a weapon has been brought to school. Safety plans support the child by considering the behaviour behind the use of a weapon and planning ways to manage any identified risks/triggers and seek support from adults and peers. They are inclusive of parents and staff and are a planned intervention to support young people in feeling secure in the school/setting, helping young people identify behaviours that may leave them feeling anxious or at risk, and have strategies they can apply to keep themselves feeling safe.

    Each case will be judged on an individual basis.

# **9. External Examinations Policies** {#9.-external-examinations-policies}

## **9.1. Malpractice Policy – GCSE and A-Level Exams** {#9.1.-malpractice-policy-–-gcse-and-a-level-exams}

### **9.1.1. Scope** {#9.1.1.-scope}

This policy ensures that all students are aware of what constitutes exam malpractice, the consequences of engaging in it, and how to maintain the highest standards of integrity during all assessments and examinations.

### **9.1.2. Terminology** {#9.1.2.-terminology}

Malpractice refers to any act that compromises the integrity of an assessment or examination. This includes behaviour by a student, teacher, or other individual that gives an unfair advantage or affects the result of the exam.

### **9.1.3. Policy Statement and Principles** {#9.1.3.-policy-statement-and-principles}

Examples of student malpractice include, but are not limited to:

*   Bringing unauthorised materials into the exam room (e.g., mobile phones, notes, smartwatches).
*   Copying from another student or allowing another student to copy.
*   Collusion (working with others on work that must be completed individually).
*   Plagiarism (submitting someone else’s work as your own).
*   Using AI tools (e.g., ChatGPT) to generate assessable content unless explicitly permitted.
*   Impersonating another candidate or having someone else take the exam on your behalf.
*   Disruptive behaviour in or near the exam room.
*   Attempting to communicate with others during an exam.
*   Altering or forging coursework or controlled assessments after submission.

### **9.1.4. Use of AI and Technology** {#9.1.4.-use-of-ai-and-technology}

*   AI-generated content (e.g., essays, code, research) must not be submitted for assessment unless specifically allowed.
*   Use of translation apps, grammar checkers, or idea-generation tools must follow teacher guidelines.
*   Failure to declare use of AI tools, or passing AI-generated work off as your own, is considered malpractice.

### **9.1.5. Procedures for Dealing with Suspected Malpractice** {#9.1.5.-procedures-for-dealing-with-suspected-malpractice}

If a student is suspected of malpractice, the following steps will be taken:

1.  Initial Investigation by the Exams Officer or relevant teacher.
2.  Collection of Evidence – this may include witness statements, copies of exam papers, plagiarism reports, etc.
3.  Formal Report will be submitted to the relevant exam board (e.g., AQA, Cambridge) if necessary.
4.  Candidate Interview – the student will be given a chance to respond to the allegation.
5.  Outcome and Sanctions – decisions may be made by the school or by the exam board, depending on the severity and nature of the case.

### **9.1.6. Possible Consequences** {#9.1.6.-possible-consequences}

Consequences of malpractice can be serious and may include:

*   A warning.
*   Loss of marks for a component or subject.
*   Disqualification from the subject.
*   Disqualification from all qualifications for that exam series.
*   Being reported to UCAS or other institutions (in severe cases).

These decisions are made by the exam board, not the school, and are final.

### **9.1.7. Student Responsibilities** {#9.1.7.-student-responsibilities}

To avoid malpractice, students must:

*   Follow all JCQ (Joint Council for Qualifications) exam regulations.
*   Attend exams on time and with the correct equipment.
*   Not bring unauthorised materials into the exam room.
*   Submit their own, original work for coursework and NEAs.
*   Follow teacher guidance regarding the appropriate use of technology and AI. Ask if unsure whether something is permitted.

### **9.1.8. Communication** {#9.1.8.-communication}

This policy will be:

*   Shared with students via assemblies, tutor time, and class time.
*   Available on Phidias for parents.

### **9.1.9. Monitoring and Review** {#9.1.9.-monitoring-and-review}

The effectiveness of this policy will be reviewed annually.

### **9.1.10. Reference Documents** {#9.1.10.-reference-documents}

## **9.2. Internal Appeals Procedure & Assessment Reviews (Including AI Misuse Awareness)** {#9.2-internal-appeals-procedure-&-assessment-reviews-(including-ai-misuse-awareness)}

### **9.2.1. Scope** {#9.2.1-scope}

This document outlines the procedures for:

*   Appealing internal assessment decisions (e.g., coursework, non-exam assessments).
*   Requesting Enquiries About Results (EARs).
*   Educating students about academic integrity, including appropriate use of AI tools.

### **9.2.2. Terminology** {#9.2.2-terminology}

*   **Internal Assessment**: Any assessment carried out by the school (e.g., coursework, non-examined assessments) that contributes to a student’s final grade.
*   **NEA (Non-Exam Assessment)**: Coursework or other assessments completed outside of formal examinations, usually under controlled conditions.
*   **Appeal**: A formal request by a student to review an internal assessment decision they believe to be incorrect or unfair.
*   **Enquiry About Results (EAR)**: A process available after external results are published, allowing students to request a review of marking, moderation, or clerical accuracy.
*   **Plagiarism**: Presenting someone else’s work or ideas as one’s own, without proper acknowledgement.
*   **Collusion**: Unauthorised collaboration with others on work that is meant to be completed individually.
*   **Academic Misconduct**: Any action that gives a student an unfair academic advantage or misrepresents their own work, including misuse of AI.
*   **AI Tools**: Software powered by artificial intelligence that can assist with writing, research, editing, or idea generation (e.g., ChatGPT, Grammarly).
*   **Academic Integrity**: The principle of being honest and responsible in academic work, ensuring all work submitted is genuinely one’s own.
*   **Exams Officer**: The member of staff responsible for managing internal and external assessments, including appeals and communication with exam boards.

### **9.2.3. Policy Statement and Principles** {#9.2.3-policy-statement-and-principles}

**Internal Appeals Process (for Internal Assessments and NEAs)**

If a student believes that an internal assessment decision is incorrect or unfair (e.g., a mark for coursework or a non-examined assessment), they may follow this process:

**Step 1: Informal Discussion**
The student should first speak with the subject teacher to understand the rationale behind the mark or decision.

**Step 2: Formal Appeal Request**
If the issue is not resolved informally, the student must submit a formal appeal in writing to the Exams Officer within 5 school days of receiving their mark. The appeal must clearly state the grounds for the appeal (e.g., marking error, incorrect application of assessment criteria).

**Step 3: Review Panel**
A panel of staff not involved in the original assessment will review the student’s work and the marking process. The student will be informed of the outcome within 10 school days.

**Step 4: Appeal Outcome**
The outcome will be communicated in writing. This decision is final regarding internal processes. Appeals must be concluded before final marks are submitted to the exam board. Any appeals made by parents must also be done in writing and will be kept on file for 5 school years or until final results are issued for GCSEs / A levels.

### **9.2.4. Enquiries About Results (EARs) – External Assessments** {#9.2.4.-enquiries-about-results-(ears)-–-external-assessments}

After the release of external exam results (e.g., GCSEs, A-levels), students can request a review through the EAR process.

**How to Request an EAR:**

*   Students should speak to the Exams Officer or their subject teacher to discuss whether an EAR is appropriate.
*   Written consent must be provided by the student (or parent if under 18).
*   Requests must be submitted within the deadline set by the exam board (usually within 2–3 weeks after results day).
*   The school may charge a fee unless the review leads to a change in the result.

**Types of EARs include:**

*   Clerical check.
*   Review of marking.
*   Review of moderation (for coursework).

**N.B.** Marks being moderated may also go down if the examination board reassesses the work.

### **9.2.5. Academic Integrity & AI Misuse** {#9.2.5.-academic-integrity-&-ai-misuse}

**Student Awareness**

At the beginning of the academic year, all students receive clear guidance on academic integrity, including:

*   What constitutes plagiarism, collusion, and misconduct.
*   The responsible use of technology, including AI tools such as ChatGPT, Grammarly, etc.

### **9.2.6. AI Usage Policy** {#9.2.6.-ai-usage-policy}

Students are informed that AI tools:

*   May be used for planning, brainstorming, and revision — if permitted by the teacher.
*   Must not be used to generate or complete assessable work unless explicitly authorised.
*   Must be acknowledged if used as a source in any part of a submission.

**Consequences of Misuse**

Misuse of AI or plagiarism in coursework or assessments will result in:

*   Referral to the Academic Misconduct Panel or Head of Year.
*   Possible disqualification from the assessment or disciplinary action, depending on severity.
*   In external assessments, exam boards may be notified if malpractice is suspected.
