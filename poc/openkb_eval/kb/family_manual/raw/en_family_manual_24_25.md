# 1. Our School

## 1.1 Imagine Montessori School Valencia

Imagine Montessori School Valencia is a private, co-educational (mixed-gender education), and secular school that follows the Montessori curriculum at all levels, as well as the National Curriculum of England and Wales, adhering to the standards and guidelines of the UK Department of Education (DfES, Department for Education and Skills).

It is recognised and authorised by both the UK Department for Education (DfES) and the Spanish Ministry of Education.

Imagine has integrated Montessori pedagogy with the British national curriculum, taking a creative and hands-on approach to learning. This results in a wonderful combination that caters to the interests and needs of our students.

The teaching team consists of English-speaking teachers with international educational experiences, bringing a broad and enriching cultural perspective to the pedagogical content.

Our educational project is designed to support the development of our students at a psychological, physical, and emotional level, to nurture their minds. To achieve this goal, we offer a carefully prepared environment that promotes their innate curiosity and fosters their autonomy, creativity, love of nature, and sense of belonging to a community.

This handbook serves as a guide for families, providing information on our faculty, organisation, and the operation of our school.

## 2.2 School Hours, Punctuality Guidelines and Drop-Off and Pick-Up

**School Opening Hours:** 8:00 a.m. - 5:30 p.m.
**Reception Hours:** 8:45 a.m. - 1:00 p.m. and 2:30 p.m. - 4:55 p.m.
**Arrival Time:** 8:45 a.m. - 9:00 a.m.
**Departure Time:** 4:40 p.m. - 4:55 p.m.

**The Importance of Punctuality**
We encourage families to arrive before 9:00 a.m., as:

*   Guides personally greet each student between 8:45 a.m. and 9:00 a.m., creating a welcoming atmosphere that encourages students to begin their activities proactively.
*   Arrivals after 9:00 a.m. disrupt the flow, as students who arrived on time have already started their activities, and guides are engaged in presenting materials. This may cause a late-arriving student to feel disoriented or unsettled.
*   Punctuality fosters a focused and productive environment, supporting students in developing autonomy and a sense of responsibility, while building positive habits for their future growth.

**EARLY PICK-UPS** –

For pick-ups, we ask that families or legal guardians arrive 5 minutes before the designated pick-up time.

As pick-ups outside the set schedule disrupt class dynamics and affect students' workflow, the only available pick-up times are as follows:

*   12:00 p.m.
*   2:30 p.m.
*   4:00 p.m.

Early pick-ups will take place at Reception.

**ABSENCES OR CHANGES IN ARRIVAL OR DEPARTURE TIMES** – Please notify in advance any student absences to:

*   By sending a Phidias message to the administration and the student’s Guide or tutor.
*   By calling the school from 8:45 am in cases of unforeseen circumstances.

Similarly, any changes in school arrival/departure times (e.g., medical appointments) should be reported, so they can be taken into account when planning daily activities and/or student logistics.

**LATE ARRIVALS** – Students who arrive late significantly disrupt the learning environment and the education of other students. When students arrive late, they miss essential instructions given at the beginning of the day, which can significantly impact their performance, regardless of their academic ability. Additionally, students may feel uncomfortable entering the classroom when others are already settled.

Late arrivals also interrupt the entire class and the teaching staff, compromising the learning experience for everyone.

Starting from the fifth late arrival during the school year, **a fee of €5 per late arrival will be charged on a monthly basis.** Information about late arrivals is recorded on a form; if a high frequency of late arrivals is detected, the Guides will arrange a meeting with the family and the teaching team to assess how we can support the family.

**LATE PICK-UPS** – If, for any reason, families arrive for pick-up after 4:55 pm, a "late pick-up fee" will be charged on the corresponding monthly invoice. The fees according to the delay are as follows:

*   From 4:55 pm to 5:15 pm – 5 € per late pick-up
*   From 5:16 pm to 5:30 pm – 10 € per late pick-up
*   From 5:31 pm to 5:45 pm – 20 € per late pick-up

# 3. Communication Between Families and the School

At Imagine, we believe that smooth communication between families and the school is crucial. We aim for this communication to be swift and dynamic, as we know it contributes to the well-being of the students and the peace of mind of the families. To facilitate this, we use several communication platforms such as Phidias and Transparent Classroom.

The language of communication will be:

1) Administrative and secretarial matters: in Spanish and in English.
2) Pedagogical matters: in English with a link to the Spanish translation. The rationale for providing a link to the Spanish translation of pedagogical emails in English is to assist Spanish-speaking families in understanding the content, while allowing the teaching staff to dedicate the maximum amount of their time to activities related to the children rather than translating texts.

**Phidias** is our communication tool between our pedagogical team or administration and our families. Through Phidias, families have access to folders with relevant documents from the school, such as the monthly menu, photos, school calendar, yearbooks, and more. In each user’s personal space, you can access payments, the “pick-up authorisation” process, and the “Imagine Shop” for purchasing school materials.

For secondary students, Phidias will also be used for the grading and reporting system.

**Transparent Classroom** is a pedagogical communication tool used in the Infant Community, Children’s House, and Primary sections where our pedagogical team will share observations about the students and other weekly activities. It is a **one-way** communication channel from the teaching staff to the families.

The flow of communication from the school to families will also include the following channels:

**Individual Meetings with Families**: These meetings take place at the end of the first and third terms. The Guide provides a summary of the student's progress to the family, along with a written report, which will be sent via Transparent Classroom or Phidias, depending on the stage, before the meeting so that families can address any questions during the meeting.

**Newsletter**: The purpose of this periodic newsletter is to share with families the most relevant events in the school community.

**Daily Communication**: Guides in the Infant Community and Casa de Niños are available at drop-off and pick-up times to discuss anything families wish to share. For Primary and Secondary, communication with Guides will be via Phidias.

**Tutorials**: Each Guide has designated weekly tutorial hours to address specific issues throughout the year. You can request these directly from your Guide via Phidias.

For any administrative matters, you can contact our administration team directly through Phidias.

# 5. Our Curriculum

Our primary goal is to provide a modern education that meets the objectives of the British curriculum while adhering to the methodology and philosophy of Maria Montessori. This approach fosters student independence and encourages a positive attitude towards learning. Our classrooms are organized and adapted to ensure that all materials are completely accessible to students, with furniture and shelves designed to their height, as well as work materials and accessories. All educational materials and the method of presentation are specifically designed to teach, demonstrate, and enable students to practice concepts independently. Once a student has been guided to work with a material and has received the appropriate presentation from the Montessori guide, they can continue to work with that material independently, as they already know how to use it. The practice and repetition of activities allow students to acquire skills and achieve goals independently. As guides introduce new materials, students have more variety to use during their Work Cycle, which will enhance their knowledge and develop/refine their skills.

Our materials are sensory and facilitate practical, hands-on learning, making the transition from concrete to abstract concepts smoother. Our teachers are qualified and experienced in using the Montessori method within a British curriculum environment. Additionally, we provide opportunities for the professional development of our staff.

As a British school in Spain, we follow the guidelines set by the Department for Education and Skills (DfES). The Montessori philosophy and the UK educational curriculum are aligned, teaching and emphasizing the same curricular goals, concepts, and skills. Both approaches aim to reinforce children's natural curiosity and desire to learn, helping them become inquisitive thinkers who include and consider the opinions of others.

## 5.1 First Years – Early Years

The Early Years Foundation Stage (EYFS) and Primary Department’s primary goal is to provide students with a holistic education that aligns with our commitment to offering a school experience that fosters self-confidence, emotional, personal, and social development, and encourages independent learning and, most importantly, enjoyment.

In the Infant Community, the main focus is to ensure that students build a solid foundation within the three core areas of learning:

*   Personal, Social, and Emotional Development (PSED)
*   Physical Development (PD)
*   Communication and Language (C&L)

Once students demonstrate confidence and skill in these three areas, the guides begin to delve into the Specific Areas of Learning:

*   Literacy (L)
*   Mathematics (M)
*   Understanding the World (UW)
*   Expressive Arts and Design (EAD)

We address the seven EYFS learning areas within the Montessori Work Cycle, as well as through integrated themes and the creation of the following clubs: yoga, gardening, sensory play, crafts, music, drama, and storytelling.

We firmly believe that each student is an individual with unique strengths, potential, areas for development, interests, and learning styles. We work with each student at their own pace and allow them to engage with different concepts at a level that provides the right challenge for their age and abilities. In each classroom, students of three different ages interact, which promotes cooperation and reduces competition. Children are assessed within the seven EYFS Learning Areas, which align with the five Montessori learning areas.

Some examples of activities associated with each of the five Montessori areas are:

**SENSORY AREA**
Our sensory activities allow students to gradually refine each of their senses individually, which indirectly prepares them for later work in language and mathematics. As students develop their skills by classifying and sorting objects according to different characteristics, they enhance their ability to perceive and understand their environment.

**CULTURAL AREA**
We use a wide range of materials that allow students to gain knowledge and appreciation in subjects such as botany, music, science, geography, zoology, history, and art. Additionally, we plan activities that provide them with hands-on experiences.

**PRACTICAL LIFE**
By working on practical life skills, students learn to perform the tasks they see around them daily. For example, children serve their own lunch and also serve others. They are taught precise movements to help them use serving spoons, cut food, and dress independently. They are frequently given small guidelines on behavior and courtesy, teaching them how to navigate and interact with others in society.

**LANGUAGE**
The development of language skills begins with auditory discrimination games. Children are introduced to phonetic sounds using sandpaper letters. Once they have mastered these sounds, they start reading with Montessori materials, which gradually enhance and perfect their reading abilities.

Students also learn grammar and make progress in writing from the very beginning. This starts with writing on a board, using the movable alphabet, and progresses to writing on paper, copying texts, and free composition. Throughout this process, emphasis is placed on refining letter formation and positioning.

Additionally, language skills are reinforced during 'Circle Time' with activities such as 'Show and Tell,' which further supports their verbal communication and self-expression.

**MATHEMATICS**
We will use sandpaper numbers and become familiar with quantities using the We will use sandpaper numbers and become familiar with quantities using number rods. Operations such as addition and subtraction are covered using both abstract and concrete materials. All knowledge of the number system arises from the concrete manipulation of Montessori materials such as rods, beads, spindles, cubes, and counters. Tens, hundreds, and thousands are also incorporated using concrete materials, which help facilitate the visualization of the number system with these same Montessori materials.

## 5.2 Primary (6-12)

Students in Primary are assessed using the National Curriculum standards. The subjects of the National Curriculum continue to be covered within the Montessori work cycle.

Children between the ages of 6 and 12 enter a new stage of development. The child's absorbent mind evolves into a more conscious mind. Primary students show a keen interest in understanding reasons, explanations, and the concept of time. They begin to form their own ideas and concepts and are eager to explore these further.

Primary students are motivated to work in groups and start to organize themselves not only for companionship but also as a means of structuring their work. Our classrooms support social development through mixed-age groups, while Montessori guides observe and supervise the process carefully without interfering.

Aligned with an environment and teaching method that promotes cooperative work and exploration, the Montessori curriculum harnesses the natural sensitivity of this stage to energize Primary education. Each school year, students are introduced to five "Great Lessons" (as defined by Maria Montessori) through interpretation and specific materials such as timelines. These lessons provide a framework to which students add details, their perspectives, and understanding throughout their years in Primary. These stories include:

1.  History of the Universe
2.  History of Life on Earth
3.  History of Humanity
4.  Mathematics
5.  Language

These stories outline the curricular structure and encourage students to delve into traditional subjects, specifically those covered by the National Curriculum (such as science, history, geography, mathematics, and language and literature), with increasing depth each year.

Grammar, spelling, verb tenses, mathematics and its operations, and geometric constructions are all covered in the Montessori curriculum, benefiting from the students' natural curiosity and their constant desire to explore the world. These skills are strengthened with materials and exercises that continue from the sequences introduced in the Infant Community and Children’s House environments. The sequence of materials gradually leads students towards abstraction and paper-based work. The Montessori curriculum promotes deep understanding, creative thinking, problem-solving, teamwork, and skill refinement.

In addition to the traditional content covered by Montessori and aligned with the National Curriculum standards, we enhance STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics) content through a cross-curricular workshop called “Creative Lab.” In this workshop, these disciplines are integrated into projects carried out by the students. These subjects play a crucial role in today’s society, and we equip students with the ability to interact with them in a hands-on manner.

# 6. Psycho Pedagogical and Career Guidance Department

**Psycho Pedagogical Guidance**

At Imagine, we believe that every student is unique and experiences different circumstances and/or specific conditions. This is why we consider the role of a psychologist to be essential in supporting students and their families, ensuring they gain the maximum benefit from their educational experience.

The Psycho Pedagogical Guidance Department is responsible for:

*   Identifying potential special educational needs in students.
*   Advising families on the type of external specialist they should consult for a diagnosis.
*   Coordinating with external specialists and supporting teachers in making the necessary adjustments to daily routines, as well as defining action plans to be implemented collaboratively at school and at home with strong coordination.
*   In certain cases, the school may determine that individualised support is required for the child’s benefit. If so, families will be informed about the type of support recommended (part-time/full-time, shared/individual) and the initial suggested duration. This additional service will be included in the monthly educational fees.

It is important to note that our department does not conduct diagnoses or therapeutic interventions directly; rather, it serves as a link and provides continuous support in the student’s educational journey.

**Career Guidance**

The Career Guidance Department is responsible for:

*   Vocational counselling: Helping students identify their interests, skills, and aspirations to make well-informed decisions about their academic and professional future.
*   Providing information on educational and career options: Offering insights into universities and career pathways.
*   Developing employability skills: Supporting students in CV writing, interview preparation, and job search strategies.
*   Guiding decision-making: Providing personalised advice so that students have the necessary information to choose the options best suited to their needs and goals.
*   Liaising with businesses and professionals: Facilitating career guidance experiences, including talks, company visits, and internship programmes.

# 7. Nutrition

## 7.1 Encouraging the Enjoyment of Healthy Eating

Nutrition is a central pillar in student development, and its impact extends far beyond health and well-being. It has a direct connection with our body, as well as with the science and biology that provides natural curricular links. At Imagine Montessori School Valencia, we provide meals made with high-quality ingredients. Our goal is to expose our students to a variety of tastes, textures, and aromas that contribute to a nutritious and healthy diet.

## 7.2 Characteristics of Our Food

Our meals and snacks are nutritionally balanced and are made daily from scratch using organic, seasonal, and locally-sourced ingredients whenever possible. We do not use processed foods with added fats or sugars, and all meals are free from artificial colourings and preservatives, and are low in salt. The water we use is filtered and available in the classrooms every day, reducing the need for bottled water and eliminating the use of plastics.

We adapt our dishes to medical needs certified by a specialist (gastroenterologist and allergist) and offer a variety of menus to accommodate the needs of our community. All our food is prepared daily on-site by our chefs at Imagine, with passion for nourishing the bodies and minds of our students and staff.

## 7.3 Educational Approach

Students eat together in their classrooms, and everyone helps before and after the meal. Students set the table daily using ceramic plates and metal cutlery.

We believe each student should have the opportunity to understand firsthand where their food comes from, how it is prepared, and how waste can be used productively. With this in mind, students take care of our gardens, manage our compost bin, and collect recyclable materials throughout the year. Depending on the season, vegetables, herbs, or fruits that students have planted, tended, and harvested are prepared and incorporated into the day's lunch.

Our gardening programme aligns closely with Montessori philosophy, offering real experiences with seeds, soil, water, and sunlight, as well as the life experience involved in growing food. As a result, students experience the process of where their food comes from and build a natural understanding of how food is linked to the land, its seasonal cycles, and the care required.

# 9. Health Standards

## 9.1 Medications and Illnesses

It is essential that students do NOT attend school if they are ill or in the incubation period of an illness, as this poses a risk of contagion to both staff and classmates. When students are unwell and therefore unable to participate in class activities, they need to be isolated from the group while waiting for their family members to collect them.

In line with the recommendations of the Spanish Association of Paediatrics, students may only return to class after a **minimum of 24 hours** have passed since the last episode of fever, vomiting, or diarrhea. This allows time for recovery and helps prevent the spread of illness to teachers and classmates.

Below are some cases where a child should not be brought to school (this is not an exhaustive list):

*   Fever: If the child has had a fever, even if it has decreased after taking an antipyretic, or if they have had a fever in the last 24 hours (37.5ºC/99.5ºF or higher).
*   Vomiting: If the child has vomited in the last 24 hours.
*   Diarrhea: If the child has had diarrhea in the last 24 hours.
*   Streptococcal Pharyngitis: The child must be on antibiotic treatment for at least 24 hours before returning to school.
*   Cold or Flu: If the child has a runny nose and/or cough, especially if these symptoms have caused them to wake up during the night.
*   Head Lice or Nits: The child should stay home until treatment is completed.
*   Intestinal Worms: If the condition is not contagious, the child may return to school after the first cycle of treatment, provided a medical note confirms that the type of parasite is not contagious.
*   Rash: If the child has a rash.
*   Conjunctivitis: The child must be on antibiotic treatment for at least 24 hours before returning to school.

Imagine staff will not administer medications unless the Phidias tracking form specifically for this case is completed:

[How to authorize the administration of medication through Phidias](https://vimeo.com/1003109908/a6c73524dc)

Medications should not be given to students or placed in their bags but should be handed directly to the school office or the bus monitor, along with the properly completed and signed authorisation form.

If medication is required every 8 hours or more, families should adjust the schedule to administer it at home. For safety reasons and according to the guidelines from the Legal Service of the Ministry of Education, **our school will only administer oral medications, always with a medical prescription and written authorisation from the families.**

If a student shows symptoms of incubation or illness, or has head lice or intestinal parasites, parents will be asked to keep the student at home until the prescribed treatment is completed.

If a student becomes ill at school and the staff deems that they cannot participate in scheduled activities or may be contagious, the family will be contacted to collect the student **as soon as possible**. If we are unable to reach the family, we will contact the emergency person listed on the student's file.

If no contact can be located and an emergency arises, we will proceed to call an ambulance.
